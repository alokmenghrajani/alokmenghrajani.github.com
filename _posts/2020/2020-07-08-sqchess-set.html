Blender is a free and open source software for 3D modeling. Supported platforms include Windows, Mac, and Linux. In order to learn some basics of Blender, I revisited an old project of mine from a few years ago which involves designing and 3D printing a chess set.

One way to learn Blender is to watch Youtube tutorials. For example, Blender 2.8 Beginner 3D Modeling Tutorial explains basic Blender user interface elements as well as basic modeling techniques. Those basic techniques are enough for modeling a king, bishop, and pawn. Modeling with curves: wires and pipes gave me the knowledge to model the knight by explaining how to use Bezier curve. Finally, the rook and queen required creating cuts in the model, a skill I learned by reading Objects & Mesh Editing A Simple Character In Blender.

I decided to make my pieces roughly the same height as a standard Staunton set. My pieces are Square-ish, so I aimed for equal base surface area. Chess sets typically come with weights, so I poked some holes (using boolean modifiers). I used the following table, based on my personal measurement, as a guide:

I initially didn't think about rendering a final image in Blender (since my end goal is 3D printing the pieces). I figured I'd still give it a shot and I'm quite happy with my textures but I definitely haven't mastered the lighting aspect of things. The board I modeled is inspired by Making an end grain chessboard, a video showcasing beautful craftsmanship.

Since I don't own a 3D printer, I explored various printing services: shapeways.com, i.materialise.com, 3dhubs.com, xometry.com, craftcloud3d.com, and sculpteo.com. The prices varied by over a factor 5x, so make sure to shop around if you ever need a 3D printing service! Obviously the material you pick plays a significant role, with PLA being among the cheapest and resin/metals being much more expensive. If you are 3D printing at home, make sure to properly vent the room where you print — you don't want to be breading any toxic fumes!

Chess pieces typically use lead (Pb) or ferrite to add weight to them. I decided to explore tungsten (W) since lead is toxic. Tungsten is also among the densest materials available, resulting in nicely weighted pieces. For reference, tungsten's density is 19 g/cm³ while lead is about 11 g/cm³, bismuth 10 g/cm³, stainless steel 8 g/cm³, and common magnets are typically 5-8 g/cm³ depending if ferrite, neodymium, or samarium-cobalt is used.

Tungsten is however difficult to work with; I don't have the necessary tools to cut, sand, or shape it. I decided to use Alibaba to contact tungsten suppliers in China. I asked for quotes for 5mm rods cut to 20mm and 30mm lengths. I received various quotes, ranging from $85 to $200. I picked the cheapest supplier since the material won't be visible.

Total time+money: ~1 week to learn Blender, ~2 days to model the pieces, ~1 day to compare prices across 3D printing services, ~1 day to contact tungsten suppliers, $85.46 for printing pieces, $88.95 for weights (yes I paid more for the weights than the pieces!). $0 for glue and felt I already had lying around. Overall, the chess set turned out fairly expensive (I could have purchased a giant lawn set for the same price) but it's the price to pay for custom manufacturing ¯\_(ツ)_/¯.

I achieved my goal of learning some basic Blender skills and being able to manufacter something without leaving my home feels magical!
